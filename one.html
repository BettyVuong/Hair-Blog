Overview

When learning about hairstyling, people don't expect there to be more than the application of techniques. But being a hairstylist means being well-versed in hair theory. Understanding the anatomy, structure, and properties of hair is very important. Understanding the anatomy of the skull is important for understanding application areas, technique markers, and more. When looking into hair colouring, it is also an art meaning that colour theory is vital to the craft. The amount of theory in hair and hairstyling took me by surprise, and I'm assuming it did for you as well. I'll be summarizing some key ideas and making concise notes in this article but I encourage you to learn more on your own using the Milady Standard Cosmetology by Catherine M Frangie textbook.

Hair Structure

A strand of hair has three layers, the cuticle, cortex, and the medulla. The cuticle is the outermost layer of the hair, it has a fish scale like layer that overlaps hair cells. The cuticle protects the inner layers of the hair. The cuticle is tight and close to the cortex to maintain the integrity of the hair. The cortex is the next layer. It is composed of this fibre-rich protein that has cells containing melanin. Melanin is a pigment that provides hair its colour. There are two types of melanin, eumelanin and pheomelanin. The difference is that eumelanin has brown and black pigment while pheomelanin has red, orange and yellow pigment. Keep this in mind for the discussion on hair levels. The cortex is also responsible for the elasticity of your hair, which is vital since our hair would snap off easily otherwise. Lastly, the medulla is the innermost layer of the hair. The medulla is typically only present in darker, coarser, or thicker hair and is not relevant in hair theory.

Hair Composition

Hair is really rich in protein, its chemical composition can be denoted as COHNS, which is carbon, oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, and sulfur. Proteins are chains of amino acids which are bonded by peptide bonds. This is called a polypeptide chain. The cortex is composed of a lot of polypeptide chains there are three types of bonds that hold the chains together to form the cortex. They are called side bonds and are responsible for the health, elasticity, and strength of the hair.

Hydrogen Bonds are the weakest bonds and are broken every time they encounter water or heat. The bonds are reformed when the hair is cooled and dry. Typically, these bonds are broken when you style your hair.

Salt bonds are weak and are dependent on pH, thus they are broken by alkaline and acidic solutions, which is important for hair colouring products.

Disulfide bonds are strong and are very difficult to break.

Hair Colour

In the hair industry, hair colour is classified by a level system. This maintains a standard that can easily be communicated and identified by hairstylists and hair product companies. The levels determine how light the hair colour is, the darkest colour is level 1, and the lightest colour is level 10. Although there is a debate within the industry if a level 10 is valid!

Remember the talk about the two types of melanin? Since hair pigment is composed of melanin, the contributing pigment is the warmth in the hair that composes the colours that we see. For example, level one hair contains both pheomelanin and eumelanin which is why its contributing pigments look red-brown. While level 9 hair only contains pheomelanin which is why the contributing pigments are yellow.

When we "bleach" or lighten hair using chemicals, what we are doing is removing the melanin from the hair. The measure of how much melanin is removed from the hair and the levels the hair increases by is called lift. Certain lighteners that are colour (such as hi-lift or permanent) have a pH level that can lift the cuticle to remove the melanin pigments. Bleach has a lower pH, penetrating the cortex even further by swelling up the cuticle cells. This allows for the bleach to remove more pigment and sometimes destroy the protein composition which is why we see hair melt off if not under expert supervision. Thus, it makes sense that when darker hair gets lightened, it tends to be very warm, think about how much pigment has to be removed to get it to a higher level!
